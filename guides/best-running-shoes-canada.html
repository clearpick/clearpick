Best Running Shoes in Canada: Ranked by What Runners Actually Report (2025)

Best running shoes in Canada — Hoka, Brooks, ASICS, and more ranked by real runner experiences. What Canadian runners say about durability, fit, and whether they would buy again.

Published 2026-06-21 by ClearPick

This guide ranks the best running shoes available in Canada in 2025 — road running shoes and trail/hiking footwear — using ClearPick scores built from owner sentiment across Amazon.ca reviews, Reddit running communities, and specialty running publications. Rankings reflect what Canadian runners, hikers, and casual joggers report after logging hundreds of kilometres in each shoe.

Canadian terrain spans wet Pacific coast trails, frozen Ontario pavement in winter, and mixed Rockies conditions — factors that affect shoe selection in ways that reviews from US-centric sources may underweight. This guide addresses Canadian-specific use cases including winter wet-weather running and trail conditions common across BC, Ontario, and Quebec.

#1 Best Road Running Shoe — HOKA Clifton 9

Why it ranked

The HOKA Clifton 9 earns the top road running ranking with a 9.2 ClearPick score — the highest of any shoe in this guide. The 29mm heel foam stack and Meta-Rocker geometry combine maximal cushioning with active forward propulsion, making it the benchmark daily trainer for high-mileage runners and anyone with joint sensitivity. At 227g for a size 9, it's the lightest maximal-cushion shoe available, avoiding the lumbering feel of earlier high-stack designs.

What owners love

Joint protection on long runs is the most frequently cited owner benefit: "I have knee issues that end most long runs at 15km in other shoes. In the Clifton 9, I've run 22km without knee pain. I'm not exaggerating when I say these changed my running." The Meta-Rocker geometry earns specific praise from experienced runners: "The rocker geometry is real. The shoe doesn't just cushion — it actually rolls you forward. Felt genuinely easier at km 18 than at km 12 in my previous shoes." High-volume training users specifically call out the recovery advantage: "Running 80km/week. Retire the Cliftons at 700km and they still have life left but the cushioning is different. Worth it — my recovery is better than any other shoe I've trained in."

Most common complaint

Midsole compression rate is the most documented limitation — the foam noticeably loses its cushioning character after approximately 600km of use, earlier than some competitors: "The foam noticeably loses its cushioning character after 600km of use — earlier than some competitors. High-mileage runners replace them more frequently than they'd like." The Clifton 9 is not suited for speed work or track sessions — "The thick foam stack creates a disconnected ground feel during track intervals." A small percentage of buyers report sizing runs slightly short, recommending a half-size up.

Canadian-specific notes

The HOKA Clifton 9 has no waterproofing — it's a mesh road shoe designed for dry to light-rain conditions. Canadian runners who train through wet fall and winter conditions report that the mesh dries quickly after wet runs, but foot temperature drops in sustained cold. For winter running below approximately -5°C on pavement, many Canadian runners add a separate pair of weather-resistant shoes to their rotation. The HOKA Clifton 9 is best suited as a three-season road training shoe in Canadian climates.

Best for

Road runners with 40km+ weekly mileage who prioritize joint protection and cushioning comfort on easy miles, recovery runs, and long slow distance. Not the right shoe for overpronators, speed work, or trail running.

Canadian price: Approximately $189 CAD on Amazon.ca. Available at Running Room Canada, Sport Chek, and REI Canada.

#2 Best Daily Training Shoe — Brooks Ghost 15

Why it ranked

The Brooks Ghost 15 earns a 9.1 ClearPick score as the most consistent daily trainer in this guide. The DNA LOFT v2 midsole balances plush cushioning with enough response to remain comfortable across the full range of easy and moderate training paces. At 800–1,000+ km reported lifespan, the Ghost 15 delivers the best cost-per-kilometre value in this guide. Wide width availability (narrow, standard, wide, extra-wide) makes it accessible to runners that most shoes exclude.

What owners love

Longevity and reliability are the Ghost 15's signature owner attributes: "On my third pair of Ghost. I've tried Asics, New Balance, Saucony — I keep coming back. Logged 900km on my last pair before retiring them. These just work." Race training suitability gets consistent mention: "Finished my first half marathon in these. My feet felt genuinely good at km 18, which has never happened with any other shoe I've trained in." Wide width options are specifically valued: "The wide width option is the reason I buy Brooks. My foot is too wide for most shoes and the Ghost wide actually fits. No squishing, no blisters." The DNA LOFT v2 improvement over Ghost 14 is noted by returning buyers: "DNA LOFT v2 is noticeably softer than the Ghost 14. The ride quality at easy paces is excellent."

Most common complaint

The Ghost 15 is built for neutral runners — owners who need stability report it doesn't provide sufficient medial support: "Neutral-only design doesn't work for overpronators. Several buyers returned them after finding they needed the Adrenaline GTS." Speed work incompatibility is the second most common complaint: "The plush cushioning that makes the Ghost great for easy miles makes it feel sluggish during track workouts and tempo runs." The 8mm heel-to-toe drop can cause calf tightness for runners transitioning from low-drop shoes.

Canadian-specific notes

The Brooks Ghost 15 is a road-specific shoe and performs on pavement, packed paths, and groomed gravel — common Canadian running surfaces in spring through fall. The rubber outsole handles light rain and damp pavement adequately but offers no waterproofing. Canadian runners who run through winter should be aware that road grip in icy or slushy conditions is limited — the Ghost 15 is not a winter running shoe. Brooks' 90-day wear test return policy applies to Canadian purchases through authorized retailers including Running Room.

Best for

Neutral runners who want a reliable daily trainer for easy miles, long runs, and moderate-pace training through the Canadian running season (approximately May through November in most markets). Ideal first running shoe for new runners.

Canadian price: Approximately $159 CAD on Amazon.ca. Available at Running Room Canada and Sport Chek.

#3 Best Trail and Outdoor Shoe — Salomon X Ultra 4 GTX

Why it ranked

The Salomon X Ultra 4 GTX earns a 9.0 ClearPick score as the best three-season outdoor footwear option for Canadian conditions. The GORE-TEX membrane keeps feet dry through stream crossings, dewy grass, and sustained rain — conditions Canadian hikers in BC, Ontario, and Quebec navigate throughout spring, summer, and fall. The Contagrip MA outsole is specifically tuned for mixed terrain: wet rock, soft dirt, and packed trail in the same day, which is the standard Canadian day-hiking condition rather than the exception.

This shoe is classified as a hiking shoe rather than a running shoe — it is included in this guide specifically for Canadian outdoor lifestyle buyers who use a single shoe for trail hiking, light outdoor running, and everyday outdoor activity. It is not a road running shoe and should not be compared to the HOKA Clifton 9 or Brooks Ghost 15 on road performance.

What owners love

GORE-TEX waterproofing performs under sustained Canadian wet conditions: "Hiked 600km in the Rockies over two summers in these. GORE-TEX never failed — stream crossings to the ankle, sustained rain, morning dew grass. Zero wet feet." Contagrip MA outsole performance on wet rock earns the most enthusiastic owner quotes in this category: "The grip on wet rock is incredible. Hiked a scramble route in BC after a night of rain that would have been sketchy in any other shoe. Contagrip bites into granite like it's dry." The Quicklace system earns consistent converts after initial scepticism: "Quicklace took one hike to get used to. Now I can't go back to traditional lacing — the cinch takes 2 seconds and the tension is perfectly even."

Most common complaint

GTX breathability reduction in summer heat is the top complaint: "Feet run significantly warmer in the waterproof version than in the non-GTX variant on hot summer days. Several buyers wish they'd bought the non-GTX for their primarily dry-season hiking." Quicklace cord replacement requiring proprietary Salomon parts bothers some buyers long-term. The break-in period is also noted: "The midsole and upper take 8-12 hours of hiking to fully break in." Sizing recommendation from owners is consistent: go half a size up from your standard size.

Canadian trail context

The Salomon X Ultra 4 GTX is disproportionately popular among Canadian outdoor buyers compared to US or European markets. The extended wet seasons in BC and Atlantic Canada, mixed-condition Rocky Mountain trails, and spring shoulder season conditions across Ontario and Quebec all favour GORE-TEX waterproofing as a practical year-round investment rather than a niche feature. Salomon's Canadian distribution is well-established — available at MEC, Sport Chek, Atmosphere, and Amazon.ca.

Best for

Three-season day hikers in Canada who navigate mixed wet and dry conditions, outdoor lifestyle buyers who want a single shoe for hiking, light trail use, and travel. Not suited for road running, winter use (no insulation), or technical trail running.

Canadian price: Approximately $249 CAD on Amazon.ca. Available at MEC Canada and Sport Chek.

What to Look For When Buying Running Shoes in Canada

Heel-to-toe drop for knee and ankle health

Drop (heel-to-toe height differential) is one of the most biomechanically significant shoe specifications. Higher drop (8–12mm) like the Brooks Ghost 15 and Salomon X Ultra 4 GTX suits runners coming from traditional footwear. Lower drop (4–6mm) like the HOKA Clifton 9's 5mm is accessible to most runners but requires adaptation for those coming from higher-drop shoes. Very low or zero-drop shoes require gradual transition to avoid calf and Achilles strain. If you are transitioning between drop categories, do so gradually over 4–6 weeks of mixed mileage.

Cushioning for cold pavement

Canadian pavement is hard, and in cold weather it becomes more so — lower temperatures reduce the compressibility of asphalt and concrete surfaces. Higher-stack cushioning shoes like the HOKA Clifton 9 provide meaningful impact protection benefit on cold Canadian pavement for runners with joint sensitivity. This is a Canadian-specific case for maximal cushioning that is less relevant in warmer climates where pavement retains more elasticity.

Durability and kilometre lifespan

Running shoe lifespan varies significantly. The Brooks Ghost 15 consistently reports 800–1,000+ km. The HOKA Clifton 9's foam softens noticeably after approximately 600–700km. At Canadian retail prices ($159–$189 CAD), cost-per-kilometre works out to approximately $0.20–0.30/km — a useful frame for comparing shoes at different price points. Higher-priced shoes do not always deliver proportionally longer lifespans.

Width options for Canadian buyers

Foot width is frequently underserved in running shoe retail. Both the HOKA Clifton 9 (wide width option) and Brooks Ghost 15 (narrow, standard, wide, extra-wide) offer meaningful width options. The Salomon X Ultra 4 GTX is available in a single standard width — buyers with wider feet consistently recommend sizing up half a size. Running shoes that don't fit in width cause blisters, black toenails, and early abandonment regardless of other attributes.

Canadian terrain considerations

Road shoes (HOKA Clifton 9, Brooks Ghost 15) are optimized for pavement and packed surfaces — they underperform on muddy, wet, or rooted trails. The Salomon X Ultra 4 GTX is specifically built for mixed Canadian terrain. Buyers who run on both roads and trails should consider two pairs rather than compromising: a road shoe for pavement training and a trail shoe for outdoor routes. MEC's trial period and Running Room's return policy make this comparison shopping accessible in Canada.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best running shoe for wet Canadian winter conditions?

None of the road running shoes in this guide (HOKA Clifton 9, Brooks Ghost 15) are designed for snow, ice, or sustained cold-weather running — they're three-season road shoes. For winter running specifically, Canadian runners typically add a pair of trail-hybrid shoes with more aggressive outsole lugs and optional running spikes (such as Yaktrax). The Salomon X Ultra 4 GTX with GORE-TEX handles wet and shoulder-season cold well but is not insulated for sustained winter cold.

Should I size up in HOKA Clifton 9 or Brooks Ghost 15?

For the HOKA Clifton 9, a portion of buyers — particularly those with feet at the longer end of their standard size — recommend going half a size up. For the Brooks Ghost 15, most buyers find their standard size accurate; buyers with wider feet should order the wide-width version in their standard size rather than sizing up. For the Salomon X Ultra 4 GTX, going half a size up from your standard size is the most consistently recommended approach in Canadian owner reviews.

Are these shoes available at Canadian retail stores?

Yes. HOKA Clifton 9 is available at Running Room, Sport Chek, and select DSW Canada locations. Brooks Ghost 15 is available at Running Room Canada, Sport Chek, and online. Salomon X Ultra 4 GTX is available at MEC (Mountain Equipment Company), Sport Chek, Atmosphere, and Amazon.ca. All three are also available on Amazon.ca with Prime shipping to Canadian addresses.

Which shoe is best for a first half-marathon in Canada?

Owner data supports the Brooks Ghost 15 as the most reliable choice for a first half-marathon — the DNA LOFT v2 cushioning handles the approximately 18–21km distance comfortably, the durability means training miles don't degrade the race-day shoe prematurely, and the smooth heel-to-toe transition reduces fatigue in the second half of the race. The HOKA Clifton 9 is also strongly rated by owners for race distances — joint protection benefits are particularly valued by runners who experience knee or hip discomfort in longer efforts.

How does the Salomon X Ultra 4 GTX differ from a trail running shoe?

The Salomon X Ultra 4 GTX is a hiking shoe, not a trail running shoe. At approximately 380g it is significantly heavier than dedicated trail runners (typically 250–300g), and its midsole is tuned for stability and protection on rough terrain rather than responsiveness for running. Owners who attempt to run in it consistently report the weight as a limiting factor. For trail running specifically, Salomon's Speedcross or Ultra Glide series are the appropriate alternatives. The X Ultra 4 GTX excels at hiking, all-day trail use, and multi-terrain outdoor activity.
