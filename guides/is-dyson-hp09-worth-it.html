Is the Dyson Purifier Hot+Cool HP09 Worth It? What Owners Report After a Full Year

Is the Dyson HP09 worth its premium price? Real owners report on whether it purifies air, heats rooms, and cools effectively or whether cheaper alternatives do the same.

Published 2026-06-21 by ClearPick

The short answer

The Dyson HP09 Purifier Hot+Cool is worth it for a specific buyer: someone who needs genuine air purification, supplemental heating, and a fan in one unit, has limited floor space, and won't flinch at approximately $849 CAD upfront plus roughly $70 per year in filter costs. For everyone else — particularly buyers who want the best performance per dollar from any single function — separate devices win on value every time. Owners at the 12-month mark are split almost evenly between "best purchase I've made for my home" and "I could have bought something better for less."

What owners love

The most frequently reported benefit — mentioned in the majority of long-term reviews — is floor space consolidation. Owners in studios, condos, and one-bedroom apartments describe replacing three separate devices with one unit, and several note that the psychological benefit of a less cluttered room is underrated. The HP09 genuinely replaces all three devices functionally rather than compromising on each.

The real-time air quality LCD display is the second-most praised feature among owners who've had the unit for 6+ months. Owners describe watching PM2.5 spike during cooking and adjusting the fan speed accordingly — behavior they say they've maintained long after novelty wore off. Several allergy and asthma owners specifically report that the ability to see air quality data made them use the purification function more actively, not just leave it on auto.

Heating precision is the third consistent praise point. The 1°C thermostat increment is frequently contrasted with coarser thermostats on cheaper space heaters. Owners report setting 21°C and having the unit hold 21°C rather than cycling through a 2–3°C band. For light-to-medium heating in a bedroom or home office, this precision is genuinely appreciated.

Owners who came from older Dyson models or no purifier at all frequently cite filter life and the catalytic formaldehyde filter as a point of satisfaction. The formaldehyde filter doesn't need replacement — it continuously destroys formaldehyde catalytically — and owners note this distinction as meaningful given rising awareness of off-gassing from furniture and flooring.

The most common complaints

Price versus separate devices is the dominant complaint and comes up in roughly half of critical reviews. The calculation owners do at purchase — a Coway air purifier around $200, a Vornado heater around $100, and a tower fan around $80 — totals approximately $380 for separate best-in-class options versus approximately $849 for the HP09. Owners who made this calculation and still bought the HP09 typically justify it with floor space, aesthetics, or the Dyson name. Those who regret it typically wish they'd run the math more carefully.

Filter replacement cost is a consistent secondary complaint among long-term owners. At approximately $70 per year for the HEPA and activated carbon combination (the formaldehyde filter doesn't need replacement), the HP09's annual running cost is significantly higher than Coway or Levoit alternatives. Over five years, filter costs alone approach $350, which owners note erodes the price premium argument for choosing Dyson over separate devices.

Fan cooling performance disappoints a meaningful minority of owners — most frequently those who bought the HP09 primarily expecting strong cooling airflow. The HP09 prioritizes sealed filtration and quiet heating over raw fan output, and owners in hot climates or who expected Dyson Cool-level airflow report the fan is underwhelming at high summer temperatures. The HP09 is not positioned as a primary cooling fan, but buyer expectations don't always reflect this.

MyDyson app data privacy comes up less frequently than price complaints but with notable consistency in tech-aware owner communities. The app transmits air quality and usage data to Dyson's cloud servers. A subset of owners — particularly in r/privacy discussions — describe this as a dealbreaker they didn't anticipate, or a reason they use the device without the app entirely.

Noise at settings above 5 or 6 (out of 10) is mentioned by a recurring cohort of owners in smaller bedrooms. At low settings the HP09 is genuinely quiet — owners frequently cite overnight use as a benefit — but at mid-to-high settings the airflow noise is described as noticeable enough to disrupt sleep or conversation. Most owners settle on a max comfortable setting of 4–5 for overnight operation.

The long-term reality

At 6–12 months, the HP09 story splits into two clear camps. Owners who settled on primarily using it as an air purifier with the heating as a bonus report high satisfaction — the air quality display keeps them engaged and the 360° sealed filtration system maintains effectiveness. These owners typically describe the price as justified by the quality of the purification alone.

Owners who expected primary heating or primary cooling performance report decreasing usage over time. The heater is described as effective in rooms up to roughly 12x14 feet in light conditions; in larger rooms or when trying to heat from cold, owners note the 1800W output is inadequate on its own without central heat running. Several long-term owners describe eventually supplementing with a dedicated space heater in winter despite having the HP09.

App engagement drops sharply after the first few months for most owners. Roughly 12 months in, the majority report they primarily use the remote control or voice commands rather than the app. The app is described as well-designed but not necessary for daily use after initial setup. Owners who actively track air quality data long-term are a minority — but they tend to be among the most satisfied overall.

Filter replacement at the annual mark is frequently described as a shock even among owners who knew about the cost at purchase. Dyson's replacement filters are not sold at discount retailers, and owners describe them as harder to find in Canada than expected. Several mention purchasing filters directly from Dyson.com and noting shipping times as inconvenient when the app notifies them to replace.

Who it's worth it for

Studio and one-bedroom apartment owners with less than 800 square feet who want genuine air purification and supplemental heating without dedicating floor space to multiple devices consistently report the highest satisfaction. The consolidation benefit is most concrete for this group.

Allergy and asthma sufferers in Canadian cities report specific benefit from the sealed HEPA H13 filtration — multiple owners describe measurable improvement in morning symptoms, particularly during wildfire smoke events in summer. These owners are among the most likely to continue filter replacement and report the annual cost as justified.

Home office users who want precise temperature control in a smaller dedicated workspace — typically 10x12 to 12x14 feet — report the 1°C thermostat precision as a genuine productivity benefit. Several describe it as the best desk-side heating option they've found.

Buyers already in the Dyson ecosystem who have a brand preference and want consistency in their home devices tend to report higher satisfaction than first-time Dyson buyers who were primarily price-sensitive.

Who should skip it

Buyers primarily concerned with price-to-performance across all three functions should buy separate devices. A Coway AP-1512HH, a Vornado MVH, and a basic tower fan collectively cost roughly half of the HP09 and arguably outperform it on their individual primary functions.

Owners in large rooms above 600 square feet who want meaningful heating should look at dedicated 1500–2000W space heaters with higher output. The HP09 is rated for up to 600 sq ft air purification but owners consistently report the heating is most effective in rooms 150–200 sq ft or smaller.

Buyers wanting powerful summer cooling should look at a dedicated Dyson Cool or tower fan. The HP09's airflow at high settings is meaningful but not equivalent to a device optimized purely for airflow volume.

Privacy-conscious buyers uncomfortable with usage and air quality data stored on manufacturer cloud servers should either skip the HP09 or plan to operate it without the app, which limits some of the smart functionality.

The price question

At the 6-month mark, owners who primarily use the HP09 as a purifier consistently describe the price as fair or better than expected. Those who use it primarily for heating are more likely to describe it as expensive relative to what it delivers. The annual filter cost of approximately $70 is the friction point that most clearly divides long-term satisfaction: owners who budgeted for it feel neutral about it; owners who didn't factor it in report feeling locked into an ongoing cost they didn't fully price at purchase.

Owners who explicitly calculated total cost of ownership — approximately $849 upfront plus approximately $350 in filters over five years, totalling approximately $1,200 — most often describe it as reasonable for a premium purifier that also heats. Owners who made a less detailed purchase decision are more likely to describe the ongoing cost as a disappointment.

Compared to separate devices at the same quality tier — not budget options but genuinely good separate purifier, heater, and fan — the price gap narrows over 3–5 years. Several owners specifically make this calculation when justifying renewal of the filter subscription or recommending the unit to others.

Final verdict

The Dyson HP09 earns high owner satisfaction in a specific use case: small-to-medium spaces where consolidating three devices into one is genuinely valuable, and where air quality monitoring drives ongoing engagement with the unit. For that buyer, the premium is justified and the 12-month satisfaction rate is high. For buyers who want maximum performance per dollar from any single function — the cleanest air, the most heat output, or the strongest fan — separate best-in-class devices at roughly half the price are the more rational choice. The filter cost at approximately $70 per year is not negotiable and should be factored into any purchase decision.
