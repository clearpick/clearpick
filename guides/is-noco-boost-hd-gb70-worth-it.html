Is the NOCO Boost HD GB70 Worth It? What Canadian Drivers Say After Winter Use

Is the NOCO Boost HD GB70 worth it for Canadian winters? What owners say about cold-weather jump starting, battery longevity, and whether it worked when they actually needed it.

Published 2026-06-21 by ClearPick

Is the NOCO Boost HD GB70 Worth It? What Canadian Drivers Say After Winter Use

The short answer

For owners of diesel trucks, large-displacement gas vehicles, motorhomes, and boats in Canada, the NOCO Boost HD GB70 is worth it — with one condition that matters specifically in this climate: you must warm it up before use below -20°C. At approximately $199 CAD, it is not cheap, but owners of large-displacement engines report it delivers on the promise when they actually need it. Passenger car owners are almost universally better served by the smaller GB40 at $50 less. The GB70 earns its premium among heavy-duty vehicle owners who have actually used it in an emergency — which is the real test for any jump starter.

What owners love

  • It works on diesel trucks in Canadian winter — with preparation. The most meaningful owner reports come from Prairies and northern Canada drivers who have used the GB70 in genuine emergencies at -20°C to -28°C. Multiple owners report successful cold starts on 6.7L Cummins and Power Stroke diesels after warming the unit inside a coat or inside the cab for 5 minutes first. This preparation step is critical and non-negotiable in extreme cold — owners who skipped it report reduced performance. Owners who followed it report the unit worked on the first try.
  • UltraSafe spark-proof connection eliminates connection anxiety. The UltraSafe system — which detects polarity and voltage before allowing current flow — appears in owner reports more consistently than any other feature. The practical benefit: you cannot cause a battery explosion by connecting the clamps in the wrong order. Owners frequently mention this protecting family members or teenagers connecting clamps incorrectly. The unit simply refuses to activate until polarity is correct. For non-mechanically-confident users, this is a genuine differentiator over cheaper units.
  • 2.3 lbs fits where lead-acid starters cannot. The weight difference between the GB70 and a traditional 18-lb lead-acid jump starter like the Clore JNC660 is the difference between something that lives in the glovebox and something that gets left in the garage. Owners consistently report that the GB70 stays in the vehicle because it physically fits where it needs to go. A jump starter that's at home when your battery dies has a 0% success rate. The portability is the feature that makes everything else matter.
  • USB-C PD recharging from standard chargers. Owners who travel or work remotely frequently cite the USB-C recharging as practically important. Recharging from a laptop charger, car USB-C port, or standard phone brick rather than requiring a dedicated automotive charger means owners can top up the unit without any special equipment. This contributes meaningfully to keeping it charged over Canadian winter storage periods — which matters more than any spec on the box.

The most common complaints

  • Lithium chemistry degrades meaningfully below -20°C without warm-up. This is the most critical complaint for the Canadian market and it is consistent across owner reports. Below -20°C to -25°C, lithium battery chemistry loses capacity measurably. NOCO acknowledges this in the manual. Owners in northern Alberta, Saskatchewan, and northern Ontario report the unit performs poorly when taken directly from a cold vehicle and connected immediately in extreme cold. The fix — warming the unit for 5 minutes inside a coat or cab — works reliably, but it requires knowing this step exists. First-time winter users who did not read the manual have reported failure at exactly the temperatures where they most needed it.
  • Clamp cables are shorter than expected for deep engine bays. A consistent complaint across truck and SUV owners is that the included clamp cables are shorter than professional-grade starters. Batteries positioned deep in engine bays — common in larger trucks — can make clamp connection awkward without repositioning the vehicle or the jump starter. This is not a functional failure but it creates friction in the moment when you least want friction. Extension cables solve the problem but add cost and another thing to keep in the kit.
  • Premium is unnecessary for most passenger car owners. Owners who bought the GB70 for a standard passenger car with an engine under 4L consistently note the GB40's 1000 amps would have handled their needs entirely. The GB70's 2000 amps is specifically necessary for large diesels and 8L+ gas engines — for everything else it is overpowered and overpriced. This is not a product flaw but it generates genuine buyer regret among owners who did not match the product to their vehicle.
  • Charge retention over long storage periods requires vigilance. Canadian owners who store vehicles over winter report needing to maintain the GB70's charge through the storage period. Lithium cells do self-discharge over time, and a unit left uncharged for 6 months is not the unit you want when you need it in March. Multiple owners report discovering their unit at low charge during spring startup — the opposite of the emergency preparedness they bought it for. NOCO recommends charging every 3 months during storage, which is workable but requires a reminder system.

The long-term reality

At 2-3 years of ownership, the GB70 report splits clearly by vehicle type. Diesel truck and large SUV owners almost universally maintain their satisfaction — the occasions they have needed it validated the purchase, and units they have kept charged have performed reliably. The jump starter is essentially infrastructure for these owners: invisible when not needed, invaluable when needed.

Passenger car owners at 2-3 years tell a different story. Many have still never used it in an emergency — which is, in one sense, the success case — but several report feeling over-equipped and acknowledge the GB40 would have been the better buy. A recurring thread in long-term owner communities: owners who matched the product to their specific vehicle type report satisfaction; owners who bought the most powerful unit available "just in case" with a standard car are less satisfied with the cost-to-use ratio.

Battery longevity at 3+ years shows the lithium cells maintaining capacity when the unit has been stored properly (charged every 3 months, kept at room temperature when possible). Units left in vehicles over multiple hot summers without maintenance recharging show accelerated degradation. Canadian owners who bring the unit inside over winter report better long-term performance than owners who leave it in the vehicle year-round.

Who it's worth it for

  • Diesel truck owners in Canada. If you own a diesel pickup — Ram Cummins, Ford Power Stroke, GM Duramax — the GB70 is the right unit. The 2000 amps and 6L diesel rating cover the vehicle you actually own. The cold-weather performance with proper warm-up has been validated by owners in Prairie winters at -25°C and below. This is the target buyer and the product delivers for them.
  • Motorhome and boat owners. Large gas engines in motorhomes (454, 460, 8L+) and marine applications benefit from the GB70's extended capacity. Owners report the GB40 would not confidently start large gas engines that had been sitting; the GB70 cranked them immediately. For recreational vehicles where a dead battery typically means being stranded far from help, the extra capacity is worth the extra cost.
  • Fleet or mixed-vehicle households. Owners who maintain multiple vehicles — a diesel truck, a gas SUV, a snowmobile, a boat — report the GB70 as a single purchase that covers the entire fleet. At that use case, the premium over the GB40 is distributed across multiple vehicles and the effective per-vehicle cost drops significantly.
  • Drivers in northern or rural Canada who cannot reliably wait for roadside assistance. In communities where CAA response times are measured in hours rather than minutes, having a reliable jump starter is not a convenience — it is a safety consideration. For these buyers, the GB70's reliability at rated temperatures justifies the price independently of vehicle type.

Who should skip it

  • Passenger car owners with a gas engine under 4L. The NOCO GB40 at approximately $149 CAD starts any passenger car reliably and is the better value for this use case. Owners of Honda Civics, Toyota Corollas, and similar vehicles are paying $50 extra for 1000 amps they will never need.
  • Anyone unwilling to maintain the charge over Canadian winter storage. If the unit is going to live in the vehicle and be forgotten for 6 months, a lithium jump starter is not the right tool. The performance degradation at very low temperatures combined with self-discharge during storage creates a risk of failure exactly when you need it most. A traditional lead-acid starter or a CAA membership may be a more realistic option for low-maintenance users.
  • Budget buyers expecting competition with cheaper Amazon alternatives. At $199 CAD, the GB70 is a genuine premium product. Generic jump starters at $60-80 CAD claim similar peak amps but the quality of construction, the UltraSafe protection, and the real-world cold-weather reliability are not comparable. If budget is the primary driver and the vehicle is a standard car, the NOCO GB40 is still better quality than generic alternatives at a lower price than the GB70.

The price question

At $199 CAD, the GB70's value calculus depends almost entirely on whether you have ever needed a jump start in a Canadian winter. Owners who have used it in an emergency describe the $199 as trivially small relative to the situation it resolved — a tow truck call in rural Alberta at -25°C costs more and may take hours. Owners who have never needed it view the cost as insurance, and insurance is always harder to value before you need it.

The honest long-term cost comparison: owners who accounted for the ongoing charge maintenance requirement and matched the product to their actual vehicle type report consistent satisfaction at 2-3 years. Owners who bought the most powerful unit they could find without considering vehicle fit or cold-weather prep requirements report lower satisfaction not because the product failed but because their expectations were not calibrated.

For diesel truck owners specifically: the consensus in long-term owner communities is that the GB70 is the correct purchase and the price is appropriate. For everyone else, the answer starts with: what engine size do you actually own?

Final verdict

For diesel truck, motorhome, and large-displacement vehicle owners in Canada: yes. The GB70 is specifically designed for and validated by exactly the vehicles and conditions it claims to handle. The UltraSafe protection, 2000 amp capacity, and USB-C recharging make it the best lithium jump starter available for this buyer. Buy it, learn the cold-weather warm-up step, and keep it charged every 3 months.

For passenger car owners: consider the GB40 first. The $50 savings buys 1000 amps that cover any passenger car engine, the same UltraSafe technology, and the same form factor. The GB70's extra capacity only matters if you own a vehicle that needs it.

For owners in extreme cold climates considering lithium vs lead-acid: go lithium with preparation. Lead-acid performs more consistently at -30°C without warm-up, but the 18-lb weight means most people leave it at home. The GB70 in your vehicle beats the JNC660 in your garage every time.
