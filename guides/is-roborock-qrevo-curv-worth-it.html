Is the Roborock Qrevo Curv Worth It? What Owners Say About the Premium Robot Vacuum

Is the Roborock Qrevo Curv worth its premium price? What owners say about obstacle avoidance, mopping, and whether it actually beats cheaper Roborock models.

Published 2026-06-21 by ClearPick

Is the Roborock Qrevo Curv Worth It?

The short answer

For most owners with pets or mixed hard floor and carpet homes, yes — but with conditions. The Qrevo Curv earns its premium primarily through three features owners consistently cite as genuinely differentiated: 0% hair tangle on the main brush, the FlexiArm extending side brush for true edge cleaning, and the hot-water mop dock. If none of those three matter to your household, the cheaper Roborock Qrevo S covers most of what the Curv does at a lower price point. Owners who don't have pets and don't mop frequently report the price gap is harder to justify.

What owners love

The most common praise across Amazon.ca reviews and Reddit's r/RobotVacuums covers these four areas, listed by frequency:

  • Zero hair tangle is cited in the majority of pet owner reviews. Owners with two or more dogs consistently describe this as the single feature that changed their relationship with the robot. "I have not once had to cut hair off the brush" appears in multiple Amazon.ca write-ups. Vacuum Wars lab testing confirmed a 0% tangle rate across 100+ models tested — tied for first place — and the real-world owner data matches the lab result closely.
  • The FlexiArm side brush gets called out in roughly 6 in 10 detailed reviews. The consistent phrasing is some version of "actually cleans along the wall" — owners coming from fixed-arm robots describe a visible difference in the debris strip left behind. Baseboard cleaning and furniture edges are the two specific scenarios mentioned most often.
  • Mopping feedback is strongly positive among owners who run it on kitchen and dining room floors. The dock's 75°C hot-water mop wash cycle is the most-cited reason owners describe the mopping result as "actually clean" rather than "just wet." Several owners compare it favorably to a manual WetJet mop on tile and sealed hardwood — though not on grout lines or textured surfaces.
  • Mapping accuracy and initial setup reliability come up frequently as a positive surprise. Owners report initial mapping completing in one run without major errors, with accurate room-level control through the app available immediately after. Returning from multi-day trips and having the map remain accurate is noted in several Reddit threads as a quality-of-life detail that older robot vacuums didn't handle reliably.

The most common complaints

Three complaints appear consistently enough in owner write-ups that prospective buyers should weigh them specifically:

  • Obstacle avoidance is the most frequently raised criticism, appearing in roughly half of critical reviews. The Curv uses Reactive AI structured light, not a full camera-based avoidance system. In practice, owners report that flat cables, small socks, and thin charging cords are not reliably detected. The robot pushes or runs over these objects rather than routing around them. Owners who declutter before scheduled runs or use no-go zones in the app report the issue is manageable — but it requires adapting your floor maintenance habits to the robot's limitations.
  • Airflow efficiency comes up in Vacuum Wars testing as below average for the premium tier. Day-to-day cleaning scores remain strong, but in head-to-head suction comparisons against models optimized specifically for airflow, the Curv lags. Owners in general-use scenarios don't raise this in reviews, but buyers replacing a corded vacuum for deep-clean passes on carpets should be aware the gap exists in controlled testing.
  • Some owners who stepped down from the Curv to the Qrevo S cite price-to-performance ratio as their reason. In r/RobotVacuums threads from early 2026, a pattern emerges of owners returning or reselling the Curv after concluding the Qrevo S covered 90% of the Curv's functionality at a meaningfully lower price. The owners who stayed with the Curv consistently had pets or ran the mop function regularly — those two use cases are where the hardware differentiation shows up most clearly in real-world use.

The long-term reality

Early impressions of the Qrevo Curv are generally positive, and the 6-to-12-month owner data that has started appearing in longer Reddit threads and Amazon.ca reviews is mostly consistent with first impressions — which is not always the case with robot vacuums. The dock's hot-water mop system, which might seem like a gimmick at purchase, is the feature owners at 6+ months mention most often as "I didn't think I'd care about this, but it matters." Mop pad cleanliness is the specific benefit cited: the hot water and warm-air dry cycle means the pads don't sit damp between runs, and owners note the kitchen floor result stays noticeably cleaner over weeks of use.

The obstacle avoidance limitation does not appear to improve or worsen over time — it's a hardware constraint owners either adapt to or don't. At 12 months, the owners who report the highest satisfaction are those who scheduled daily or near-daily runs and adjusted their floor habits early. Owners who expected a fully set-and-forget experience with no floor prep report lower long-term satisfaction regardless of overall cleaning quality.

Who it's worth it for

  • Pet owners with high-shedding breeds. The 0% hair tangle and FlexiArm combination is the specific hardware configuration where the Curv's premium over the Qrevo S is most justified. Owners with two or more dogs or long-haired cats consistently describe the maintenance reduction as material.
  • Mixed hard floor and carpet homes where mopping matters. Owners who run the mop function on kitchen and dining room floors at least 3–4 times per week report the hot-water dock pays off in perceived floor cleanliness. If your hard floor area is minimal, the mopping upgrade is less meaningful.
  • Buyers who considered the Ecovacs X2 Omni or iRobot Roomba Combo j9+ and want a Canadian-stocked alternative. Several owners in r/RobotVacuums threads describe choosing the Curv over the Roomba after comparing obstacle avoidance failure modes — the Roomba j9+ camera system is generally rated superior for avoidance, but Curv owners who chose Roborock cite app reliability, mapping consistency, and Canadian availability as their reasons.

Who should skip it

  • Buyers without pets who don't mop regularly. The two features that justify the Curv's price premium over the Qrevo S — hair-tangle-free brushroll and hot-water mopping dock — don't apply to this buyer profile. The Qrevo S covers navigation, carpet cleaning, and general vacuuming at a lower cost.
  • Buyers who expect set-and-forget on a cluttered floor. If your home has charging cables, kids' toys, or socks regularly scattered on hard floors, the Curv's obstacle avoidance will frustrate you. Owners who need camera-based avoidance for a consistently messy floor should consider waiting for models with AI camera systems at a similar price point.
  • Buyers prioritizing carpet-only deep cleaning. Vacuum Wars places the Curv at a carpet deep-clean score of 86/100, tied 6th of 100+ models — strong, but not the highest. For a home that's primarily carpet with no mopping need, there are models at lower prices that match or exceed the Curv's carpet performance specifically.

The price question

At approximately $849 CAD, the Qrevo Curv sits at the upper end of the mid-premium segment — below Roborock's top-tier Qrevo MaxV Ultra but above the Qrevo S by a meaningful margin. Owner sentiment on value at 6+ months splits clearly by use case: pet owners and regular moppers consistently describe the price as justified or "worth every dollar." Owners who don't fall into those categories more frequently describe it as "good vacuum, hard to justify the premium." The pattern in longer-term reviews suggests the value calculation is genuinely conditional on household type rather than a universal answer.

Final verdict

The Roborock Qrevo Curv is worth it if you have pets or mop hard floors regularly — those two conditions are where the hardware differentiates from cheaper Roborock alternatives and where owner satisfaction at 6+ months is consistently high. It is not worth the premium for buyers without pets and without a regular mopping routine, where the Qrevo S covers the meaningful use cases at lower cost. If obstacle avoidance for a cluttered floor is your primary concern, neither model fully solves that problem — the Curv's Reactive AI system is an improvement over older designs but not a replacement for a camera-based system.
