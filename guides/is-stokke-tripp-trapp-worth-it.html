Is the Stokke Tripp Trapp Worth It? What Parents Who Bought It (and Did Not) Say

Is the Stokke Tripp Trapp worth its premium price? What parents say after 2 or more years -- who it's right for, who overpaid, and whether the grows with your child claim holds up.

Published 2026-06-21 by ClearPick

Is the Stokke Tripp Trapp Worth It?

The short answer

For families who plan to use a single chair from infancy through school age — and ideally across multiple children — yes, with a critical caveat on total cost. The Tripp Trapp at approximately $329 CAD is not the complete purchase: the Baby Set required for the 6-to-36-month stage is sold separately at $60–80 CAD, making the real first-use cost $390–410. Parents who budgeted for the chair price and discovered the Baby Set at checkout are the most common source of early frustration in reviews. Factor the full cost in before comparing to alternatives.

What owners love

The most consistent praise in Amazon.ca reviews and r/beyondthebump threads covers four areas:

  • Multi-decade lifespan is the most frequently cited justification for the price, appearing in the majority of 4- and 5-star reviews. The pattern in long-term owner write-ups is remarkably consistent: parents describe using the same chair for a first child, then a second, then watching the original child use it at a desk at age 10 or 12. The 136 kg weight capacity and beech wood construction mean the chair doesn't degrade over this cycle. "Best baby purchase I ever made" appears in multiple Canadian reviews specifically in the context of per-use cost calculated over years.
  • Family table integration is cited by roughly 6 in 10 parents as a benefit they didn't fully anticipate before buying. The Tripp Trapp positions a child at adult table height rather than elevated above it or off to the side. Parents in r/beyondthebump threads describe their toddler being "part of the meal" rather than separately fed — a social and developmental dynamic they describe as qualitatively different from traditional high chairs. The aesthetic integration into adult dining rooms is mentioned frequently as a secondary benefit.
  • Adjustability that tracks the child's actual development is consistently described as functional rather than marketing. The 7-position seat and footrest adjustments are cited by parents of 2-to-6-year-olds as something they actively use as the child grows, not a one-time setup feature. Proper foot support at table height gets specific mention in several developmental context reviews.
  • Cleaning gets positive comments relative to plastic high chair alternatives. The absence of fabric cushions in the base configuration means food debris falls through or wipes off. Parents who have used traditional fabric-padded high chairs before the Tripp Trapp consistently describe the cleaning comparison favorably — no stuck-in-seam food, no fabric that traps odors. (Parents who add the cushion set note cleaning is more involved.)

The most common complaints

Three complaints appear across enough reviews to be weighted as real patterns:

  • The Baby Set sold separately is the top complaint and appears in close to half of critical reviews. The chair alone is not usable for the 6-to-36-month stage without the Baby Set backrest and harness. The $329 list price becomes $390–410 minimum, and many parents discover this after ordering. Amazon.ca listings note this in the product description, but reviewers report it's easy to miss. Buyers who discover it after checkout describe the experience as feeling misled about the real cost even though the information is technically available.
  • The hard wood seat without cushions comes up as a comfort issue for toddlers at extended meals. The unpadded beech seat is described as fine for short meals but uncomfortable for toddlers sitting for 20–30 minute family dinners. The Stokke cushion set resolves it but adds $50–80 more to total cost. Parents who skip the cushion describe more squirming and meal-leaving behavior in younger toddlers. This is a real spending decision the product page doesn't foreground.
  • Total accessory cost surprises parents who add multiple stages. Beyond the Baby Set, the Newborn Set for the 0-to-6-month stage is an additional $200+. Trays, activity tables, and cushion sets for different stages each add cost. Parents who have bought the full ecosystem across all stages report total spending of $600–800 CAD beyond the chair itself. The chair's "grows with your child" framing is accurate, but the accessories required for each growth stage are not included and add up significantly.

The long-term reality

The Tripp Trapp has been in production since 1972, and the owner data on long-term durability is the clearest of any product in the premium high chair category — because there are owners who have had them for decades. The 5+ year reports are consistently positive on structural integrity: no wobble development over time, no finish degradation under normal dining conditions, no hardware failures. Parents who use the chair for a second child after a first child has aged out of it describe no meaningful wear that affects function.

The claims about the chair "growing with your child" hold up for most parents through the toddler and early school years. The transition out of the baby seat configuration into the regular chair configuration at around 36 months is described as straightforward. Use as a regular dining chair by older children is common. Use as a desk chair by school-age children appears frequently in long-term reviews. The 10-to-15-year ownership arc that appears in Amazon reviews is not a marketing outlier — it's a documented use pattern among parents who bought for their first child and are writing about their second or third.

Who it's worth it for

  • Parents planning multiple children who want one chair that carries through all of them. The amortized cost per child is the clearest value case. One Tripp Trapp shared across two children over 15+ years costs less per year than most alternatives — and it doesn't need to be replaced between children.
  • Families where the baby eats at an adult dining table. The design is optimized for this use case. Parents who sit a toddler at a separate surface or a separate height from the family meal get less of the distinctive benefit the Tripp Trapp offers over alternatives.
  • Canadian buyers who prioritize domestic retailer availability. The Tripp Trapp is consistently stocked at Well.ca, Snuggle Bugz, and Amazon.ca. Canadian parents who want a reliable local return option — rather than cross-border shipping — describe this as a practical differentiator from some European-made alternatives. The 7-year manufacturer warranty covers defects, and claims are handled through Canadian retailers.

Who should skip it

  • Parents comparing to the IKEA ANTILOP at roughly $35 CAD. Parents who tried both describe the ANTILOP as a genuinely good high chair for the 6-to-24-month stage — easy to clean, stable, lightweight, cheap enough to not stress about. If your primary use case is feeding an infant and toddler and you don't plan to use the chair past age 3–4, the value case for spending $390+ on the Tripp Trapp is hard to make. The ANTILOP doesn't grow with the child and the aesthetic doesn't integrate the same way, but it delivers core function at a fraction of the cost for the stage most parents primarily care about.
  • Parents who won't be at a family dining table daily. The chair is optimized for the shared-meal-at-table use case. Families who use a kitchen island, eat informally, or primarily use a separate baby feeding station get less of the integration benefit that differentiates the Tripp Trapp from alternatives.
  • Renters or families who move frequently. The Tripp Trapp is bulky and not designed for flat-pack transport. Parents who expect to move in the next 2 years describe it as an inconvenient object to relocate. The long-term value case assumes a stable home environment where the chair stays in one place for years.

The price question

At $329 for the chair plus $60–80 for the Baby Set, the minimum total is $390–410 CAD for the 6-month-and-up stage. Compare this to the IKEA ANTILOP at approximately $35 or the Graco Blossom at approximately $150–180 CAD. Owner sentiment on value at 2+ years is strongly conditional on whether the chair is still in active use: parents who are on year 5 or 6 with the same chair describe it as obviously worth it. Parents who stopped at the toddler stage and no longer use it describe the value as less clear in retrospect. The "grows with your child" value case requires the child to actually grow with the chair — which most Tripp Trapp owners do, but requires an upfront commitment to that use pattern.

Final verdict

The Stokke Tripp Trapp is worth it for parents who will use it continuously from infancy through school age, especially for multiple children. The durability and longevity data is well-documented across decades of owner reports — this is not a marketing claim being projected; it's a pattern of 10+ year ownership appearing consistently in Canadian reviews. Budget the Baby Set and possibly the cushion set into the total purchase cost ($390–470 depending on configuration) before comparing sticker prices to alternatives. For parents who want a reliable high chair for the baby and toddler stage and plan to stop there, the ANTILOP or Graco Blossom covers the functional need at a fraction of the cost.
