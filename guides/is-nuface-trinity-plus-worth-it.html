Is the NuFace Trinity Plus Worth It? What Regular Users Actually Report

Is NuFace Trinity Plus worth its premium price? What women who use it consistently for 3-6 months say about visible results and what those who returned it say.

Published 2026-06-21 by ClearPick

The short answer

The NuFace Trinity Plus is worth it for users who commit to the consistency requirement — 5 minutes daily for the first 60 days, then 2–3 times per week indefinitely — and who would otherwise spend on professional microcurrent sessions. For that buyer, owners consistently report visible results at 6–8 weeks that hold as long as maintenance sessions continue. For buyers who won't maintain the routine, or who expect results equivalent to professional in-office treatments, the device will disappoint regardless of price. The ongoing gel cost of approximately $35–40 CAD per month is non-optional and must be factored into any purchase decision.

What owners love

Visible jaw definition and brow lift are the two results owners describe most specifically and most frequently. At 8 weeks of daily use, a clear majority of owners in long-term review threads report photographically documentable changes — not subtle shifts that require imagination to see, but visible improvements in jaw contour and brow position that other people notice and comment on. Multiple owners describe being asked by friends, family members, or their dermatologist whether they'd "had something done."

The FDA clearance is cited frequently by owners who did comparison research. Several owners specifically note choosing the Trinity+ over competitor devices — including the Foreo Bear and various face massagers — because FDA clearance for microcurrent facial toning is a meaningful regulatory distinction, not just marketing language. Owners who did this research describe higher confidence in the investment.

The Wrinkle Reducer red light attachment is consistently praised among Trinity+ bundle owners who use it. Several describe the combined microcurrent and red light approach as producing better results than microcurrent alone in their experience, particularly around eye area fine lines. Owners note that equivalent standalone red light devices cost approximately $100–$200 CAD separately, which factors into their value assessment of the bundle.

Long-term owners who maintained the routine for 6+ months describe a cumulative benefit — not just maintaining initial 60-day results but continuing gradual improvement. Several 12-month owners report their results at 12 months exceed their results at 8 weeks, describing a compounding effect that incentivizes continued use.

The most common complaints

The gel consumable cost is the single most frequently mentioned complaint across all owner communities. At approximately $35–$40 CAD per bottle and approximately 30–60 days of daily use per bottle, the annual gel cost ranges from roughly $200–$480 CAD depending on usage. Owners describe this as poorly communicated at purchase — either buried in fine print or not prominently disclosed. Several owners specifically flag this as the reason they'd be hesitant to recommend the device to others despite being satisfied with results.

The consistency requirement defeats a meaningful portion of buyers. Owners who report returning the device or abandoning it before 60 days consistently describe the same pattern: good intentions at purchase, regular use in the first two weeks, then a few missed days, then the routine breaks down entirely. Owners on Reddit estimate — based on discussion thread patterns — that roughly a third of Trinity+ buyers don't complete the initial 60-day protocol. Without completing the protocol, owners uniformly report seeing little or no result.

Results disappear when you stop. This is the second most reported complaint at the 6-month mark. The Trinity+ delivers results that require ongoing maintenance — not permanent changes. Owners who paused for 2–3 weeks describe noticing visible regression. Several describe this as not a flaw in the device but a buyer expectation mismatch — they expected results to be more durable. Owners who understood this upfront tend to be less bothered by it.

The microcurrent sensation is described as uncomfortable by a consistent minority — particularly on bony areas like cheekbones and around the jaw. Most owners who persist past the first 2 weeks describe adapting to the sensation, and several note that starting at a lower intensity setting and building up over 2 weeks made the experience manageable. Owners who gave up describe the sensation as a contributing factor to abandoning the routine.

Charging reliability complaints appear in a subset of long-term reviews — typically 12–18 months in. Multiple owners describe the charging port becoming finicky, requiring precise cable positioning, or the device failing to hold charge. This issue is reported frequently enough to be a recognized concern rather than isolated incidents, though it does not appear in the majority of long-term reviews.

The long-term reality

At 3 months, the Trinity+ story is almost entirely about consistency. Owners who maintained daily use report consistently positive experiences and visible results. Owners who fell off the routine report wasted money. The device does not reward sporadic or casual use — it is specifically a cumulative-dose technology.

At 6 months, the picture is more nuanced. Consistent users describe shifting from the intensive 5-minute daily phase to the maintenance phase of 2–3 sessions per week, which most describe as sustainable in a way the daily protocol was not for some. Owners note that the maintenance phase feels less like work and more like part of a broader skincare routine.

Comparison to FOREO Luna is raised by a notable minority of owners who've tried both. The FOREO Luna is a cleansing device, not a microcurrent toning device — the technologies are different — but buyers who considered both describe the NuFace as delivering more visible facial toning results while the FOREO delivers better cleansing. Several owners use both. Among owners who tried both expecting comparable toning results, NuFace is consistently preferred for lifting and definition.

Results by age and skin type show patterns in owner reports. Owners in their 30s and early 40s with mild to moderate laxity describe the most dramatic results. Owners in their 50s with more significant laxity describe meaningful but more modest improvement. Owners in their 20s with minimal laxity to begin with describe seeing less noticeable change — not because the device doesn't work, but because there was less to improve. Owners with oilier skin describe the gel requiring more frequent reapplication to maintain conductivity.

Who it's worth it for

Consistent routine maintainers who already have an established daily skincare ritual are the buyers most likely to succeed. Owners who describe themselves as disciplined about skincare consistently report better outcomes than impulse buyers. If you've maintained a skincare routine for more than 6 months, you're more likely to maintain the NuFace protocol.

Buyers who would otherwise seek professional microcurrent treatments see clear financial logic. Professional in-office microcurrent sessions run approximately $150–$300 CAD per session. At the device price of approximately $489 CAD plus ongoing gel costs, most owners who would seek monthly professional treatments describe the device paying for itself within 3–5 months.

Owners in their mid-30s to mid-50s with early-to-moderate facial laxity report the most visually significant results in owner communities. These buyers see enough change to clearly photograph and validate the investment.

Buyers who'll factor in gel costs from day one tend to have better overall satisfaction. Owners who budgeted for the ongoing gel consumable feel less surprised by it at month two; those who didn't budget for it describe it as a consistent irritant even when results are otherwise satisfying.

Who should skip it

Buyers who won't maintain daily use for 60 days should not buy the Trinity+. The protocol is the product — results are cumulative and require consistent treatment. If your schedule, lifestyle, or motivation level makes 5-minute daily use for 60 days unlikely, owner data is clear: you will not see meaningful results and will likely return it.

Buyers with contraindicated conditions should not use microcurrent devices. These include pacemakers, pregnancy, epilepsy, metal facial implants, and active inflammatory skin conditions including severe acne and rosacea flares. NuFace documents these contraindications thoroughly, but owner communities note they are still sometimes overlooked at purchase.

Buyers seeking dramatic or permanent results equivalent to surgical or professional medical treatments will be disappointed. Owner results are real and documentable but fall into the category of visible improvement rather than transformation. The device tones and lifts; it does not replace professional aesthetic treatments for buyers with significant laxity.

Buyers who don't want to budget for ongoing consumables should factor in approximately $350–$480 CAD annually in gel cost before committing. Alternative conductive gels are used by some owners, but NuFace's Gel Primer is the only option validated for conductivity by the manufacturer.

The price question

At 6 months, owners who see results and maintain the routine are largely positive about the value. The most common framing from satisfied long-term users: the device costs less than 3 professional microcurrent sessions and continues delivering results indefinitely with maintenance use. At the 12-month mark, owners who are still using the device describe it as the most cost-effective premium skincare investment they've made — specifically in comparison to other devices, serums, and treatments they've tried.

Owners who don't see results — typically those who didn't complete the 60-day protocol — describe the approximately $489 CAD price as a waste. This group represents a meaningful portion of buyers based on return rate discussions. The device's value is entirely contingent on use compliance, which is a more demanding requirement than most skincare purchases.

The gel cost arithmetic is worth doing before purchase: approximately $35–$40 CAD per bottle, one bottle per 30–60 days at daily use in the intensive phase, and one bottle every 60–90 days at maintenance. Over a year, this represents approximately $200–$480 in consumables. Owners who did this math upfront are more satisfied than those who encountered it as a surprise.

Final verdict

The NuFace Trinity Plus delivers on its core promise for buyers who meet the consistency requirement. Owner data is specific and recurring: visible jaw definition, brow lift, and cheek lift at 6–8 weeks that maintain with continued use. The gel consumable cost and maintenance dependency are non-negotiable realities that must be understood before purchase. For the buyer who would otherwise spend on monthly professional microcurrent sessions, the financial case is clear. For the buyer who won't maintain daily use for 60 days or won't budget for ongoing gel, the device will disappoint regardless of how good the results could theoretically be.
