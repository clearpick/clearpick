Is the EGO Self-Propelled Mower Worth It vs Gas? What Homeowners Report After 2 Seasons

Is the EGO LM2156SP worth it over a gas mower? What Canadian homeowners say after 2 full mowing seasons -- runtime, power, and total cost.

Published 2026-06-21 by ClearPick

The short answer

The EGO Power+ LM2156SP is worth it for Canadian homeowners with lots up to approximately one-third of an acre who want to eliminate gas mower maintenance entirely, and for buyers already in the EGO 56V battery ecosystem where the mower becomes the centrepiece of a fully battery-powered yard tool setup. After two full mowing seasons, the dominant owner sentiment is positive — the brushless motor handles Canadian spring growth, the self-propel system works on moderate slopes, and the reduction in seasonal maintenance is valued beyond simple cost savings. The price is real: at approximately $849 CAD for the full kit, it costs more upfront than comparable gas mowers, and the financial break-even typically takes 3–4 seasons. Owners on lots above a half-acre or with particularly steep grades report meaningful limitations.

What owners love

The elimination of gas maintenance is the most universally praised benefit among two-season owners. No pull cord, no oil changes, no stale gas in the carburetor, no winterization, no spring tune-up. Owners describe this as the feature that remains consistently valued long after novelty wears off — particularly the instant-start push-button operation that requires zero preparation. Multiple owners specifically describe the pull cord frustration with previous gas mowers as a significant quality-of-life improvement by comparison.

Quiet operation comes up constantly in owner reviews across Amazon.ca and Reddit communities. Owners describe mowing at early morning hours — before 8 AM — without disturbing neighbours, which is a practical benefit that owners on tighter schedules or with specific mowing windows report as genuinely life-improving. Canadian suburban lot density makes this more relevant than it might be in rural settings.

The brushless motor's handling of thick spring growth is the third most consistent positive report. Canadian spring grass — particularly after a wet May or after letting a lawn go an extra week — is notoriously demanding. Owners describe the LM2156SP handling this without the bogging and stalling that they experienced with lower-powered battery mowers or older gas mowers with carb issues. This is the performance claim that has held up best in two-season owner reports.

Battery ecosystem value for existing EGO 56V owners is explicitly praised. Multiple owners describe the mower purchase as completing a battery-powered yard tool lineup — blower, trimmer, and mower all sharing the same 56V Arc-Lithium battery platform. These owners consistently describe the highest overall satisfaction, framing the mower price in the context of eliminating the need for separate battery platforms for different tools.

The most common complaints

Runtime on larger lots is the most specific complaint from second-season owners. At approximately 45–60 minutes per charge on the included 7.5Ah battery, most owners on lots of one-third to one-half acre complete their lawn in one charge with some margin. Owners on lots approaching half an acre report cutting it close or needing occasional top-up charges, which requires planning around the 90-minute standard charge time. Owners on lots over half an acre consistently describe the runtime as insufficient for a single-session mow without a second battery.

Battery and charger confirmation at purchase is the most reported purchasing friction. Some Amazon.ca listings show the tool-only version while others include the full kit with battery and charger. Owners who purchased expecting the full kit and received only the mower describe significant frustration — the 7.5Ah battery alone costs approximately $150–$200 CAD. This is a listing presentation issue rather than a product defect, but it recurs frequently enough in reviews to warrant explicit attention before clicking purchase.

The 21-inch deck generates a recurring complaint from owners with lots above a third of an acre who feel they spend more time on extra passes than they would with a 22-inch deck. The difference is one inch, but on a longer mowing session it accumulates. Owners describe this as a minor frustration rather than a dealbreaker, but it's mentioned consistently enough to be a real consideration for larger lots.

Wet grass and mulching performance is a specific Canadian concern that a minority of owners flag. In wet Canadian spring and fall conditions — dewy mornings, post-rain mowing — owners describe the mulching mode producing clumping rather than clean dispersion. Most owners switch to bag or side discharge in wet conditions, but some who bought primarily for mulching report disappointment with wet-grass mulch quality.

Winter battery storage concern is mentioned by a cohort of Canadian owners approaching their second or third winter. EGO recommends storing batteries at room temperature between 15–25°C, charged to approximately 30–60%. Owners who followed this guidance report no capacity degradation after two winters. Owners who left batteries in an unheated garage report noticeably reduced runtime in the second season. This is user-dependent but comes up frequently enough in Canadian owner discussions to be worth calling out explicitly.

The long-term reality

At the end of two full mowing seasons, the EGO LM2156SP holds up well in owner communities. The motor and deck show normal wear but no reported failures among the majority of two-season owners. The variable-speed self-propel dial remains responsive. Several owners specifically note the lack of any mechanical issues that they'd be attending to with a gas mower at the two-year mark — no carb cleaning, no blade sharpening service, no belt replacements.

Gas-versus-electric total cost calculations at two years consistently favour the EGO for owners who factor in gas, oil, tune-ups, and pull cord replacements. Owners who did this calculation at purchase typically estimate break-even between year 3 and year 5 depending on their previous gas mower's maintenance costs. Owners who didn't factor in maintenance costs when comparing purchase price are more likely to describe the EGO as expensive even at two years.

Third-year owners who surface in Reddit discussions report two distinct concerns: blade replacement (standard maintenance, similar to gas mowers) and battery capacity. Most two-year owners report no detectable battery degradation. A small subset of three-year owners who used batteries heavily across multiple tools report modest capacity reduction — roughly 5–10% reduced runtime — which they describe as acceptable for normal degradation. No significant battery failures are reported in the owner communities reviewed.

Who it's worth it for

Owners of lots up to one-third of an acre report the most consistent satisfaction with a single-charge mowing experience. This covers the majority of urban and suburban Canadian lots. Owners in this category rarely mention runtime as a concern.

Buyers already in the EGO 56V ecosystem — particularly those with the EGO blower or trimmer — describe the mower as the purchase that completed their outdoor power tool setup and allowed them to eliminate the last gas tool from their lineup. These owners report the highest overall satisfaction and the clearest sense of value.

Homeowners who previously dealt with gas mower reliability issues — hard starting, carb problems, winter storage headaches — consistently describe the EGO as a quality-of-life improvement that exceeds the financial comparison. The instant-start, no-maintenance operation is described as transformative by owners who dealt with unreliable gas mowers.

Buyers sensitive to mowing noise — either due to early-morning schedules, neighbourhood density, or personal noise sensitivity — describe the quiet operation as one of the most meaningful real-world benefits after two seasons of use.

Who should skip it

Owners of lots significantly over half an acre will struggle with runtime limitations on a single 7.5Ah battery. A second battery extends range but adds approximately $150–$200 to the total investment and requires planning around charging. Owners with large lots who prioritize uninterrupted mowing should look at gas-powered alternatives or EGO's higher-capacity battery upgrade.

Buyers with very steep grades report that the self-propel system works adequately on moderate slopes but becomes less comfortable on steep inclines — particularly during turns. Owners with significant grade describe the mower as heavy (approximately 82 lbs with battery) and note that manoeuvring uphill on steep terrain is more demanding than a lighter gas mower.

Buyers primarily focused on low upfront price should note that comparable gas self-propelled mowers from Honda or Toro are frequently available in the $500–$650 CAD range. The EGO's financial case is a long-term total cost argument, not an upfront cost argument. Buyers who won't own the mower for 3+ seasons may not reach the financial break-even point.

Owners who store tools in unheated garages or sheds in Canadian winters should plan battery storage carefully. Lithium batteries should be stored indoors at room temperature. This is a real constraint in Canadian winters that owners who don't have indoor garage storage or basement access need to account for.

The price question

At the two-season mark, satisfied EGO LM2156SP owners describe the value proposition clearly: no gas, no oil, no tune-ups, no pull cord frustration, lower noise, and a motor that handles Canadian spring growth. When they calculate what they were spending annually on gas (approximately $60–$100 per season depending on lawn size), oil changes (approximately $20–$30), and tune-ups or repairs (variable, but approximately $80–$150 every 2–3 years for most gas mowers), the break-even against the $849 upfront cost falls around year 3–4 for most. Owners who add in the time value of not doing spring maintenance or dealing with starting issues often reach break-even earlier in their personal calculation.

Owners who are dissatisfied at two seasons typically cite either runtime limitations on larger lots or the buyer expectation that the mower would perform identically to gas in all conditions including very wet or very overgrown grass. The mower handles typical overgrowth well but struggles in truly demanding conditions that a high-powered gas mower handles more easily.

Second-battery owners — those who bought an additional 56V battery to extend range — describe the addition as the change that made the mower feel genuinely unlimited for their lot size, but note that the second battery brings the total investment to approximately $1,000–$1,050 CAD, which changes the financial calculus significantly.

Final verdict

After two full Canadian mowing seasons, the EGO LM2156SP earns its position as the benchmark battery self-propelled mower. The brushless motor handles spring growth, the self-propel works on moderate slopes, and the elimination of gas maintenance is valued more with each season. The 45–60 minute runtime is genuinely sufficient for most Canadian lots up to one-third of an acre; it becomes limiting above that. Buyers in the EGO 56V ecosystem or buyers who prioritize maintenance-free operation and quiet running over maximum upfront value are the clearest beneficiaries. For buyers with half-acre-plus lots or genuinely steep grades, gas alternatives remain more practical. The financial break-even against a comparable gas mower typically comes around year 3–4 — owners who plan to keep the mower for 5+ years consistently describe the purchase as justified at the two-year mark.
