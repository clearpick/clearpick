    Best Cordless Leaf Blower in Canada 2026 — EGO vs Greenworks vs Ryobi

    Cordless leaf blowers have crossed a threshold where they're genuinely as powerful as many gas models — without the maintenance, fumes, or pull-cord frustration. But picking the best cordless leaf blower canada 2026 means cutting through a lot of spec-sheet noise. Here's what actually matters, and which blower wins.

    CFM vs MPH — What These Numbers Mean

    Every leaf blower lists two air figures: CFM (cubic feet per minute) and MPH (miles per hour). They measure different things, and marketers often highlight whichever looks bigger for their model.

    • CFM (volume): How much air the blower moves per minute. This is the more important number for moving large piles of wet, heavy leaves. High CFM is what actually shifts material.
    • MPH (speed): How fast the air travels. High MPH is useful for dislodging stuck debris from cracks and edges, but a high-speed, low-volume blower is like a powerful jet of air from a straw — it moves one spot at a time.

    For Canadian yards, where you're dealing with maple, oak, and birch leaves that can pile up and get damp quickly in autumn, you want high CFM. A blower with 600+ CFM will clear a yard dramatically faster than a 300 CFM model, regardless of MPH.

    Alternatives Worth Considering

    Greenworks (40V / 60V models)

    Greenworks offers competitive blowers at generally lower price points than EGO. The 60V models approach EGO's performance numbers, and if you're already in the Greenworks battery ecosystem (which is wide and well-supported in Canada), a Greenworks blower makes sense. The tradeoff is that EGO's battery platform tends to hold higher resale value and has a more robust tool selection for growing into.

    For budget-conscious buyers who just want to clear leaves and aren't invested in a battery ecosystem yet, Greenworks is a sensible starting point.

    Ryobi (40V HP models)

    Ryobi's 40V HP lineup is the pick if you're already in the Ryobi ONE+ or 40V ecosystem — and many Canadian homeowners are, since Ryobi tools are widely sold and among the most affordable battery-platform options. The Ryobi 40V HP blower delivers solid performance for average yards and typically costs less than the EGO.

    Where Ryobi falls behind is in raw power at the top end. If you have a large yard, heavy leaf fall, or frequently deal with wet leaves, EGO's higher CFM output will save you significant time per session.

    Canadian Winter Storage Tips

    Lithium-ion batteries don't like extreme cold, and Canadian winters can damage batteries stored improperly in unheated garages or sheds.

    • Store batteries indoors at room temperature during winter months. Even a basement is better than an unheated garage.
    • Store batteries at 40–60% charge, not fully charged or fully depleted. Most modern EGO batteries have indicator lights to check charge level.
    • Don't charge a battery that's been left in freezing temperatures — bring it inside and let it warm to room temperature first (at least a few hours) before charging.
    • Clean the blower tube before storing to prevent debris from compacting over winter.
    • Run the blower for a minute in spring before putting it to full use to confirm all functions are working normally after storage.
