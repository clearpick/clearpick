    ✅ What Works

    • Same 4th Gen Scarlett preamp as the 2i2 at $50 less — the Solo's single microphone preamp is identical in quality to the 2i2. For solo recording artists who only ever record one microphone at a time, the Solo offers identical audio quality at a lower price.
    • Simultaneous mic + instrument recording for singer-guitarists — the Solo can record a microphone through the XLR and a guitar through the 1/4-inch instrument input at the same time. Live solo performers who want to capture voice and guitar simultaneously can do so.
    • Air mode for vocal presence — same as the 2i2, the 4th Gen Solo includes an Air button that adds transformer-style high-frequency enhancement. Particularly useful for vocals that need brightness and presence added during tracking.
    • Direct monitoring blend knob for real-time performance feedback — the blend knob lets you mix between the direct (zero-latency) mic input signal and the DAW output (which may include reverb and effects), giving performers the experience of hearing themselves with effects during recording.

    ⚠️ Worth Knowing

    • Only one XLR microphone input — the Solo cannot record two microphones simultaneously. For users who need two independent mic channels (e.g., two vocalists, mic + room mic), the Scarlett 2i2 is necessary.
    • The instrument input is 1/4-inch TS, not combo XLR — the guitar/bass input on the Solo is a separate 1/4-inch jack, not a combo XLR/TRS. This is cleaner for guitar use but means you can't use a second XLR microphone.
    • RCA outputs instead of TRS — the 2 outputs on the Solo are RCA rather than the TRS balanced outputs on the 2i2. For connecting to studio monitor speakers with balanced TRS inputs, an RCA-to-TRS cable or adapter is needed.
    • For most home recording needs, the Solo is sufficient — the vast majority of home recording hobbyists, podcasters, and home studio musicians record one source at a time. The Solo is the smarter buy unless you specifically need two simultaneous XLR inputs.
