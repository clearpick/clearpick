    ✅ What Works

    • Cast iron's heat retention is unmatched for searing — once the skillet reaches temperature, adding a cold steak doesn't drop the surface temperature the way stainless or nonstick does. The result is a deep, even sear crust that other materials don't reliably produce at home.
    • The pre-seasoning works well out of the box and improves continuously with use — the more you cook with oil, the more non-stick the surface becomes over months and years. Many owners report their 5-year pans are dramatically more non-stick than when new.
    • Induction, gas, electric coil, glass-top, oven, broiler, campfire, and grill — this skillet works on all of them without any accessory or modification. Moving from stovetop sear to oven finish is seamless.
    • With basic care (dry thoroughly, rub with a thin layer of oil), cast iron is essentially indestructible. Lodge pans are regularly passed down across generations, and the seasoning only improves with age.

    ⚠️ Worth Knowing

    • At 7.6 lb (3.4 kg), this is a heavy daily cooking pan. Owners with wrist or grip strength limitations frequently find it tiring to use one-handed over a stove — flipping or tilting the pan to baste requires real arm strength.
    • After washing, the pan must be dried immediately and thoroughly (ideally on low heat on the stove for a minute) and rubbed with a very light layer of oil before storage. Skipping the drying step even once can lead to surface rust, which is fixable but annoying.
    • Cast iron heats slowly and unevenly on electric coils compared to gas — hot and cool spots are noticeable when cooking eggs or delicate items. Gas or induction minimizes this; electric coil users may find it frustrating for precision cooking.
    • Acidic foods (tomatoes, citrus, wine sauces) cooked for more than a few minutes can strip seasoning and impart a metallic taste into the food. Long-simmered tomato sauces should stay in stainless or enamelled cookware.
